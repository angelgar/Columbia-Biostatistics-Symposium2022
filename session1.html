Wenzhao Wu

Parental exposure to pesticide and childhood retinoblastoma: a retrospective case-control study in Mexico

The current statistics has shown that among children younger than 15 who are diagnosed with cancer, 2% of them have retinoblastoma which is the most common childhood eye cancer. The goal of this study is to examine whether there is an association between parental exposure to pesticide and childhood retinoblastoma. It was a retrospective case-control study conducted in Mexico. The data were collected from 23 states, 244 municipalities and 293 localities, which included the mapping data about the percentage of cropland, and the questionnaire data related to pesticide exposures before and during pregnancy, designed separately for fathers and mothers. Among all diagnosed children, there were 122 bilateral cases and 266 unilateral cases. 140 children without retinoblastoma were included as controls. Logistic regression models were built with the outcome of developing disease and risk factors. Analyses were repeated for three case-control pairs, that is, all cases-control, unilateral-control and bilateral-control. Retinoblastoma was significantly associated with locality density of cropland (p<0.05; odds ratio [OR], 1.011; confidence interval [CI], 1.001-1.021), but not pesticide exposures. Similarly, for unilateral disease it was observed that the outcome was significantly associated with the cropland percentage under locality level (p<0.05; OR, 1.011; CI, 1.001-1.022). However, the bilateral case was not significantly associated with either the cropland percentage or the pesticide exposures. The results suggest that the locality cropland density may contribute to the development of unilateral retinoblastoma. The data of occupational and domestic exposure to pesticide might be biased due to a possible propensity of mothers filling in the questionnaire for fathers. Also, there might be a recall bias in the retrospective study. Therefore, further exploration need to be done for more informative and reliable results.

Metal Mixture and Incident Cognitive Impairment using Quantile-Based g-Computation Analysis

Background: Previous research has shown the potential linkage between trace elements and cognitive function. However, most studies focused on individual elements. This study used Qgcomp to study the joint effects of metal mixture on cognitive impairment in a cohort of US adults.Methods: The study sample included 2071 participants from the Reasons for Geographic and Racial Differences in Stroke (REGARDS) cohort. The association of the metal mixtures with the incident cognitive impairment was evaluated using Qgcomp analysis, which allows flexibility on non-directional homogeneity to examine positive and negative joint effects of metal mixture components. Among the five metals, Ca and Cd contributed positive joint effect (scaled effect size = 0.31), whereas Mg, Zn and As contributed negative joint effect (scaled effect size = -0.67).Conclusion: Findings suggest that calcium and cadmium may be harmful to cognitive function, and magnesium, zinc, and arsenic may be beneficial to cognitive health.

Environmental exposure to heavy metal contaminants: Optimizing spectrometer calibration via least squares and mixed model methods

Environmental exposure to heavy metal contaminants advances disease and mortality processes associated with cardiovascular disease. Effective biomarker measurement depends on lab-based spectrometers calibrated on standardized concentrations of geochemical elements. Calibration regresses a net signal on a standard concentration to understand the relationship between these; subsequently, this relationship is used to estimate concentrations based on samples reflecting environmental exposures. In this project, regression techniques for optimizing instrument calibration were compared for robustness,variability, and linearity. Various model specifications for least squares (OLS and GLS) and linear mixed models (LMM) were conducted on 1472 standard concentrations and their measured net signals; these data are nested in 16 elements with 12 calibration runs each. OLS results demonstrate lower measurement variability and strong linearity under instrument calibration paradigms where regressions are supplied with more standardized concentration values near the low limit of detection (LOD).Additionally, LMM analysis shows an identity relationship with OLS-estimated slopes, such that per-calibration-run OLS estimation is comparable to cross-run population-average corrected random effects, and OLS regression does not over fit to within-run error variance. Taken together, our results suggest linear extrapolation using calibration run specific OLS estimates with or without forcing the regression through the origin is a valid approach; additional calibration runs and associated data analyses suggest this can also be used to expand measurements below the previous limit of detection for this sample.
