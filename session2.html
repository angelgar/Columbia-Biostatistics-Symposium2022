Association between BMI/WHRadj and Breast Cancer Subtypes under Mendelian Randomization Methods

Previous studies have shown a significant association between obesity and the risk of overall breast cancer. However, this relationship is unclear with regards to breast cancer subtypes. Here, we performed Mendelian Randomization(MR) analysis to evaluate the link between body-mass-index(BMI) and BMI-adjusted waist-hip-ratio(WHR adj ) and five breast cancer subtypes - luminal A-like, luminal B-like, luminal B/HER2 negative, HER2 enriched, and triple negative breast cancer. Mendelian Randomization analyses were performed, using inverse-variance weighted(IVW), Median-based, MR-Egger models to assess the association of breast cancer risk and BMI/WHR adj . P-value of 0.003 was selected as the threshold to determine significant association. Luminal A-like breast cancer risk was found associated with BMI/WHR adj.

Mediation effect of white matter hyperintensities in the relationship between age and cognitive reference abilities

White matter hyperintensities are distributed patches of augmented lucency commonly found inT2-weighted MRI scans for older people. Clinically, white matter hyperintensities are related with cognitive decline and may contribute to the cause of dementia. On the other hand, a highprevalence of white matter intensities observed in older people may indicate a harmlesscomorbidity of aging. Thus, the main goal is to investigate the relationship of white matterhyperintensities and cognitive abilities with respect to age. Three types of cognitive reference abilities are considered based on previous research: memory, language, and executive functionsor processing speed.The hypothesis states that the white matter hyperintensities mediate therelationship between age and cognitive abilities. Mediation analysis is performed with the totalwhite matter hyperintensities as a single mediator as well as regional white matter hyperintensities as multiple mediators. The mediation effect of total white matter hyperintensitiesis significant in all three cognitive abilities. Occipital lobe showed the most mediation effect inall three cognitive abilities. We further investigate whether the diagnosis group moderate the meditation by comparing mild cognitive impairment group with cognitive normal group, Alzheimer’s disease group with mild cognitive impairment group. There is no significant moderation effect of the diagnosis group in the mediation path. These results support the hypothesis that white matter intensities indeed mediate the relationship between age and cognitive abilities. It further helps to clarify the role of white matter hyperintensities in the process of aging.

Colocalization of Genetic Variants from Complex and Molecular Traits

Late-onset Alzheimer’s disease is a prevalent age-related polygenic disease that accounts for50–70% of dementia cases. Identifying genetic risk factors shared between Alzheimer’s diseaseand other molecular traits, such as gene expression, can help with the understanding of theetiology of the disease and potentially detect new therapeutic targets. Colocalization analysis isan integrative analysis technique that aims to identify genetic variants introducing simultaneousphenotypic changes in multiple molecular and complex traits. This project aims to understandand utilize multiple colocalization analysis methods to identify shared causal genetic variantsbetween brain tissues and Alzheimer’s disease.Using simulated data, benchmark results for three current colocalization analysis methods,fastENLOC, eCAVIAR, and SuSiE-coloc, were produced and the methods were compared basedon false discovery and false negative numbers, power and signal cluster size. Results indicatehigher power and smaller cluster sizes for SuSiE-coloc, but high numbers of false negativesacross the board. Colocalization analysis was then performed on Alzheimer’s disease GWAS(genome-wide association study) and brain tissue eQTL (expression quantitative trait loci)summary statistics. Credible sets that potentially capture the shared causal genetic variants wereidentified, indicating a possible complex genetic relationship between Alzheimer’s diseases andthese brain tissues.The high number of false negatives generated for all three colocalization methods leads to furtherdiscussion on the sources of these errors and ways to capture the signals. The limited number ofdiscoveries also suggest a need for more accurate methods in future integrative geneticassociation analyses.

Chong Li

Impacts from Child Care, Health Insurance, and Public Benefit Program Design on Disposable Family Resources in the State of New Jersey

The “benefit cliff” refers to the phenomenon when public welfare programs have designs that let recipients earning additional income to have a net loss of financial resources, due to loss of benefits (both tangible and intangible) created by employment choices. Households’ inadvertent losses of financial resources can lead to potentially serious public health consequences for underage children, especially those who are first-generation immigrants, racial minorities and of low-socioeconomic status. This study first employs a combined-sample multiple imputation approach, using data from Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP) and American Community Survey (ACS), both sponsored by U.S. Census Bureau, to construct anonymized microdata on individual households in the state of New Jersey. The data is then plugged into the Family Resource Simulator (FRS) that performs household-level microsimulation to model potential benefit cliffs faced by individual families. Through aggregating the microsimulation outcomes, the study is able to identify common patterns of benefit cliffs facing different household types. The findings would allow legislators to implement safety net policies that better respond to the needs of families and to secure a healthier, more equitable New Jersey.

Association between Clinical Variables and Probability of Death

The data of this study comes from Nanfang Hospital of Southern Medical University located in Guangdong, China. Clinical and demographic variables such as age, blood sugar, and serum potassium were collected from 241 hospitalized patients, all women. Theclinical variables were measured once every day until the patient dies or until the study ends (6 months). The average of each clinical variable is taken for each patient. The objective is to predict the probability of death given the clinical and demographic variables. The dependent variable “Death”is a binary variable: patients who have died during the 6-month period of study are coded as 1 and patients who survivedare coded as 0. The 27 independent variables have a low correlation. Since there are too many independent variables, forward and backward elimination are used for variable selection. In addition, physicians’ decision on variable selectionis considered. Several logistic and penalized logistic regression models aretrained; the model with the highest AUC is selected to be the final model. From the logistic model trained, age, serum potassium, APS-III mortality score, platelet count, and pH of blood are significant predictors at the 5% level. Furthermore, patients with gynecological conditions and patients with comorbidities are at higher risk of death. The final model used for prediction is the Ridge penalized logistic regression model, with the best tunning parameter 𝜆= 0.02 and AUC 0.8.

Jungang Zou

Variable Selection for Multiply-imputed Data: A Bayesian Framework

Multiple imputation is a widely used technique to handle missing datain large observational studies. For variable selection on multiply-imputed datasets, however, if we conductselectionon each imputed dataset separately, different sets of important variables may beobtained. MI-LASSO, one of the most popular solutionsto this problem, regards the same variable across all separate imputed datasets as a group variable and exploits Group-LASSO to yield a consistent variable selection across all the multiply-imputed datasets. In this paper, we extend the MI-LASSO model into Bayesian framework and utilizefive different Bayesian MI-LASSO models to perform variable selection on multiply-imputed data. These five models consist of three shrinkage prior based and two discrete mixture prior based approaches. We conduct a simulation study investigating the practical characteristics of each model across various settings. We further demonstrate these methods via a case study usingthe multiply-imputed data from the University of Michigan Dioxin Exposure Study.
